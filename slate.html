Slate

Each morning it hands you a slate — about three prioritised actions, one line of rationale each — assembled from your captured matters and your calendar, and pushed to you. You run your day from it; you don't build it.

Free · Local-first · macOS
§The execution
problem

Reflection and capture aren't execution.

Inner Counsel helps you think; Docket helps you write things down. And still the open loops outrun you. The missing piece was never another place to put things — it was someone to hand you the day.

The model comes from a law office. There you don't assemble your own docket of what matters today — a paralegal does it, from the calendar and the open matters, and hands it to you. You review it; you don't build it. That's Slate. Inner Counsel helps you be better; Slate helps you do better.

A morning slate

Three things, and a reason for each.

Slate — Tuesday morning
# Today 1. Review the SailPoint access certification ↳ the LDAP cutover Thursday depends on it 2. Reply to the ADCS renewal thread ↳ rescheduled 3× — it keeps slipping 3. Draft the HOA board agenda ↳ meeting is tomorrow night

No inbox to process, no list to prune. You read three lines, and you go.

I.The defining
constraint

Zero grooming — you maintain no list.

Every todo app shares one flaw: it makes you do the gardening — process the inbox, pick today, prune the stale. Slate's constraint is the inverse: the AI gardens the backlog; you only ever see the slate.

Today is always fresh — there's no crossed-off history to wade through. Stale items aren't archived in a ceremony; they simply fade, and Slate quietly stops surfacing them. The whole product is a refusal to make you curate — because anything that needs grooming falls off around week three, and Slate is built not to.

II.Its own
app

A distinct role earns a distinct app.

Slate could have been a mode of Inner Counsel — same engine, a new persona. It isn't. The counsel and the paralegal are different people, and folding one into the other would box in the relationship with your tasks that Slate exists to hold.

So Slate is its own app, on the same logic that once made Docket its own app rather than a notes feature. The split drew the axis the family had been missing: being better versus doing better.

III.On the
seam

A reader, not a second source of truth.

Slate doesn't capture and doesn't own a database. It reads the same folder of Markdown that Docket writes and Inner Counsel reads — the structured way, through Docket's docket-mcp accessor — and assembles the day with one focused local-model call.

Its memory of what keeps slipping lives outside the vault, where Inner Counsel never looks, so nothing Slate notices loops back as if it were your own input. And the context it assembles is clamped to your machine: the model endpoint stays on loopback unless you explicitly opt into a remote one. Sovereignty by architecture, not a promise.
